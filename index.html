Toxic Work Environments

When work environments are described as toxic, there is no set definition or standard that can be referred to in order to identify the issue. In general, toxic can be any situation or conduct that has an adverse effect on an organization. Employees who experience toxic work environments have a negative psychological response that can lower their productivity as well as their life outside of work. Not only should the circumstances of the event causing these perceptions be addressed, but the employee themselves must also be emotionally cared for. Collaborative workplace yields a sense of happiness, joy, harmony, kindness, politeness, cooperation, facilitation at the workplace. Toxic workplace cause anxiety, stress, depression, health problems, absenteeism, job burnout, counterproductive work behavior, degrade productivity.

Contributing Factors

Employees can experience toxic work environments in many ways. Bullying is any form of social interaction meant to cause harm. In the workplace, this can be in the form of unconstructive criticism, ostracization, inordinate monitoring and humiliation. These harmful social interactions can come from other employees or from management. While we do have laws protecting employees against specific types of harassment (sexual, race, gender, age, etc.), bully that involves exclusion, unfair work delegation or covert action can be less concrete and can lead to employees feeling helpless or trapped in their situation. If the situation fits into the category of harassment, there are laws and mechanisms in place to help the employee address the situation. However, taking action against the organization or individual in this situation does not necessarily mean there will be an improvement in their workplace environment. The ongoing misconduct and incivility of employees starts from the top down. If a toxic workplace environment is to be improved, the whole organization must take actions to eliminate or address what is causing the toxic behavior to exist.

Burnout

Burnout is a resulting disorder generated from workplace and personal circumstances that causes employees to be emotionally disengaged in their work. Burnout can occur from the continued ostracization, bullying or harassment endured by an employee. It can also be the result of dissatisfaction with the recognition of their work that can leave employees feeling like they are invisible to management and the company or leave them feeling like they have stagnated in their career. Burnout affects the organizations productivity as well as affected employee’s mental health. It can also lead to more toxic conditions. Employees experiencing burnout can exhibit the same incivility, mood, and aggressive behavior as those causing these symptoms on them. This leads to passive or active retaliations in the workplace and further degrades the work environment.

Effects on Employees

Employees feel emotional and psychological effects from toxic work environments. Employees endure their toxic environments because the workplace is a source of survival. Employees need to work to provide for themselves or their family. Employees often evaluate their own self-worth based on the way they are treated by management. If the toxic situation being endured comes from positions of higher authority, the employee may be left feeling powerless and trapped in their situation. Being in a toxic work environment damages their ability to communicate effectively, collaborate with coworkers and focus on their work. This leads to increased levels of stress and anxiety that the employee experiences at work and carries home. After working hours, the employee can exhibit posttraumatic and mood disorders that can lead to substance abuse.

Addressing the Problem

Events and behaviors that contribute to toxic work environments lead to lost productivity in the organization, higher turnover and psychological stress. It is important to not only address the situation itself, but to support the individuals who are experiencing the toxicity. Employees may initially voice their concerns as being a “toxic work environment,” but the organization needs to identify the specific triggering event in order to be able to address the problem. The organization and management then need to evaluate if the issue is related to their general workplace culture. If the culture of the workplace perpetuates toxicity, punishing those involved in the triggering event may only serve to endanger the affected employee’s ability to continue working. Retaliation, further bullying or further isolation may occur between the employee and the workplace. If the employee is left to defend themselves in this environment, it will only serve to cause more anxiety and stress which could lead to higher turnover rates. Isolating the affected employee from the rest of the workplace should be avoided. Isolation only leads to further detachment from the rest of the workplace and does not work towards solving the underlying issues. The resolution should result in the improvement of the entire workplace. The affected employee should feel that the organization is working towards addressing the issue they are having, but if the employee decides they are not able to reconcile their issues with the workplace they should be fully supported in exiting and assisted with finding new work. Implementing educational programs about harassment and bullying can help alleviate elevated levels of these behaviors.

Conclusion

Toxic work environments reduce productivity in the workplace and is an emotional and psychological drain on affected employees. Organizations need to address situations of toxic work environments from the top down. If toxic behaviors are allowed to perpetuate, they will lead to reduced quality of life for employees and high turnover rates. The affected employees themselves should be treated and evaluated, not just the toxic situation itself. Organizations benefit greatly by creating work environments that make employees feel like part of a family, supports them should any conflicts occur and helps them grow and advance their careers.

Enhancing Work Environment

Joseph Purayidathil and Salvacion Villavicencio conducted a study using Organizational Development Intervention to enhance work environment and working relationships in the workplace. They found that employees are more likely to enjoy their jobs if they feel like they fit in and are given meaningful tasks. By having work that is not stimulating, employees will think of their jobs as unpleasant and boring. This leads to a lack of satisfaction in the job and more likelihood of increased employee turnover. Employees want to be able to feel appreciated and have the ability to grow in their career in a work environment that feels like a family. Tension and pressure cause for uncomfortable relationships between coworkers. Companies should nurture work environment that encourage open communication that is constructive and is backed up by a strong leadership group that employees can lean on if any problems arise. Organizing activities outside of work will help grow a sense of togetherness in the workplace. Employees spend eight hours a day at work so it is important that they feel that their coworkers are like a second family they can open up to and rely on for collaboration. Empowering employees to express their ideas to the company and offering training on teamwork, communication and other occupational skills gives employees confidence that they work for an employer that cares about them.
